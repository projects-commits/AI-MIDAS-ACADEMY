# CONTENT SYSTEM

## Purpose

This document defines how AI MIDAS ACADEMY content should be structured, written, reviewed, and published.

## Content philosophy

The academy teaches structured intelligence. Content should help the reader understand how to think, what to observe, how to evaluate uncertainty, and how to act with discipline.

The content should not imitate betting Telegram channels, paid picks brands, or motivational finance copy.

## Public content types

- Courses
- Lessons
- Frameworks
- Glossary entries
- Labs

## Catalog roadmap metadata

The public courses catalog may also include editorial roadmap entries before a full MDX course exists.

Rules:

- Published or draft public courses still live in `src/content/courses/`
- Roadmap-only entries live in `src/data/course-roadmap.ts`
- Roadmap entries must be clearly labeled as editorial or planned, never presented as finished teaching surfaces
- Once a roadmap item becomes a real course, the MDX course entry becomes the source of truth

## Course design standard

Every course should be:

- deep
- structured
- practical
- visually clear
- mechanism-first
- anti-hype

## Required learning ingredients

Every course should include:

- mental models
- mechanisms
- applied examples
- failure modes
- checklists
- practical exercises

These ingredients are required because the academy is designed for usable judgment, not passive reading.

## Language rules

- Primary language: Portuguese
- English technical terms may appear when they are more precise or widely recognized
- Avoid slang-heavy phrasing unless it is being defined and contextualized
- Do not write like an influencer or promotional copywriter

## Tone rules

- serious
- analytical
- direct
- practical
- research-grade

## Editorial prohibitions

Never use or imply:

- guaranteed profit
- certainty language for uncertain events
- signal-selling language
- guru posturing
- "green guaranteed"
- "lucro certo"
- "sinal infalivel"
- "aposta certa"
- "so copiar"

## Structural model

Course content should generally move through this sequence:

1. context and objective
2. core concept or mechanism
3. structured explanation
4. applied football or market example
5. failure modes and caveats
6. checklist or operating lens
7. exercise or practical task

This is a content pattern, not yet a final page schema.

## Visual clarity rules

- Long-form lessons should be broken into disciplined sections
- Dense concepts should be supported by tables, diagrams, or blackboard-style summaries when possible
- Examples should be concrete enough to inspect
- Lists should clarify thinking, not inflate content length

## Framework content

Framework pages should compress operational thinking into reusable models. They are not lightweight blog posts. They should be reference-grade and stable enough to revisit during course study.

## Glossary content

Glossary entries should define terms clearly, explain why they matter, and distinguish similar concepts where confusion is common.

## Lab content

Labs should turn theory into execution. A lab should ask the learner to inspect, compare, calculate, classify, or document reasoning rather than just read.

## Review gates

Before content is considered publishable, verify:

- conceptual accuracy
- anti-hype compliance
- structural completeness
- practical value
- visual clarity opportunities
- consistency with the academy tone
